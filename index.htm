The NES Codex

This HTML project is a compendium of technical knowledge regarding the Nintendo Entertainment System. It contains diagrams and documents outlining the system's components, programming, and operation. This codex will stand as a testament to the system's importance in the history of video game technology, and to the fact that I am a gigantic nerd for knowing all of this.

Overview

The Nintendo Entertainment System (NES) was released in 1983 in Japan (as the Family Computer, or "Famicom"), and in 1985 in the United States. Built around the 8-bit architecture of a modified MOS 6502, the system's technology was an exemplar of the era, featuring a fast (for the time) 1.79 MHz processor, a programmable sound generator, and a video chip geared towards 2D tiled graphics and sprites. But the NES also had features that were state-of-the-art for the time: a primative form of Direct Memory Access, a sound chip capable of playing back digitized recordings, and a hardware configuration specifically designed to allow for smooth screen scrolling, a feat that even the strongest PCs of the day stuggled to pull off. Its power, strong library of games, reasonable price and lack of competition ensured that Nintendo had a foothold in the hearts and living rooms of America.


Technical Specifications

CPU: Ricoh 2A03 (NTSC), Ricoh 2A07 (PAL) GPU: Ricoh 2C02 (NTSC), Ricoh 2C07 (PAL) RAM: 2KB (Expandable through RAM on Cartridges) Clockrate: 1.79MHz (NTSC), 1.66MHz (PAL) VRAM: 2KB Sprite Size: 8 x 8 pixels, 8 x 16 pixels Max Sprites: 64 (8 per hortizontal scanline) Total Colors: 54 Max Simultaneous Colors: 25 (8 palettes of 3 colors each, with 1 shared transparency) Resolution: 256 x 240 (256 x 224 in NTSC due to overscan) Audio: Onboard (via CPU) Channels: 2 Square, 1 Triangle, 1 Noise, 1 DPCM (Expandable through Cartridge Hardware)
